No One Left Behind - UNSDG 2030: Let’s Make It Happen

By Orea Kastrati

Issue I · 2026 · Essay

“No One Left Behind” is a fundamental commitment of the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (UNSDG) 2030. Yet, millions of sanitation workers, the very people who uphold public health, continue to be forgotten.

As cities awaken to clean streets, few acknowledge the laborers responsible for them. Sanitation workers toil before dawn, battling waste, disease, and danger—yet they remain invisible.

Sanitation workers form the backbone of urban health systems, keeping us clean and preventing the spread of disease. According to the International Labour Organization (ILO), over 19 million sanitation workers worldwide face daily exposure to hazardous waste, yet most lack protective equipment and access to healthcare.

Beyond direct waste exposure, many operate in confined spaces, handle toxic chemicals, or navigate unsafe sewage systems without adequate training or legal safeguards. A 2019 World Bank study identified sanitation workers in developing nations as among the most vulnerable laborers.

“After a shift, my hands itch and my chest burns from inhaling the fumes,” says Rafiq, a 39-year-old waste collector in Mumbai. Many sanitation workers endure long hours in unsanitary environments with no legal protection, earning wages that barely cover their living expenses.

The exploitation extends beyond health risks. In many regions, they are denied legal recognition, leaving them without access to government aid or social security.

True sustainability cannot exist if those who maintain public hygiene are forced to live in conditions that threaten their own health.

As Martin Luther King Jr. once said, “One day our society will come to respect the sanitation worker if it is to survive...”

The world cannot afford to leave them behind.
