No One Left Behind – UNSDG 2030: Let’s Make It Happen

By Orea Kastrati · March 2026

“No One Left Behind” is a fundamental commitment of the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (UNSDG) 2030. Yet, millions of sanitation workers, the very people who uphold public health, continue to be forgotten.

As cities awaken to clean streets, few acknowledge the laborers responsible for them. Sanitation workers toil before dawn, battling waste, disease, and danger-yet they remain invisible. Without their labor, entire societies would collapse under the weight of contamination, disease, and disorder. However, despite their essential role, these workers remain trapped in exploitative conditions, lacking proper wages, healthcare, and workplace safety.

Sanitation workers form the backbone of urban health systems, keeping us clean and preventing the spread of disease. “According to the International Labour Organization (ILO), over 19 million sanitation workers worldwide face daily exposure to hazardous waste, yet most lack protective equipment and access to healthcare.”

Beyond direct waste exposure, many operate in confined spaces, handle toxic chemicals, or navigate unsafe sewage systems without adequate training or legal safeguards. A 2019 World Bank study identified sanitation workers in developing nations as among the most vulnerable laborers, often working without access to clean water, medical aid, or labor rights.

“After a shift, my hands itch and my chest burns from inhaling the fumes,” says Rafiq, a 39-year-old waste collector in Mumbai. They suffer many work long hours in unsanitary environments with no legal protection, earning wages that barely cover their living expenses.

Sanitation workers were recognized as essential during the COVID-19 pandemic, yet they remain among the most neglected members of society. While millions quarantined indoors, these workers continued cleaning hospitals, streets, and waste facilities, often without adequate protective gear. According to a 2021 Human Rights Watch report, sanitation workers in several countries worked without masks or gloves, increasing their exposure to COVID-19 and other infectious diseases. Despite their vital contributions, they receive meager wages, lack access to basic healthcare, and often work in hazardous environments without legal safeguards.

The exploitation extends beyond health risks. In many regions, they are denied legal recognition, leaving them without access to government aid or social security. Their working conditions contradict the very principles of UNSDG Goal 3 (Good Health and Well-being), Goal 8 (Decent Work and Economic Growth), and Goal 10 (Reduced Inequalities).

True sustainability cannot exist if those who maintain public hygiene are forced to live in conditions that threaten their own health. Achieving the UNSDG 2030 agenda requires systemic reforms-fair wages, workplace safety regulations, legal protections, and access to healthcare for all sanitation workers. Nations that prioritize sanitation infrastructure must also prioritize those who sustain it.

As Martin Luther King Jr. once said, “One day our society will come to respect the sanitation worker if it is to survive, for the person who picks up our garbage, in the final analysis, is as significant as the physician, for if he doesn’t do his job, diseases are rampant.”

The world cannot afford to leave them behind.
