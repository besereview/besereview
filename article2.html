War Without Containment: The Expanding Conflict Between the United States, Israel, and Iran

By Orea Kastrati

Issue I · 2026 · Analysis

The current conflict between the United States, Israel, and Iran represents a critical shift in the nature of modern warfare. What has long existed as a shadow confrontation—defined by indirect regional forces, covert operations, and strategic ambiguity—has now moved into direct and open escalation. This transition marks not only a regional crisis, but a moment with potentially global consequences.

The initial strikes, carried out through coordinated military operations targeting Iranian infrastructure and leadership, signaled a departure from containment toward confrontation. In response, Iran’s retaliatory actions—ranging from missile deployments to the activation of allied groups across the region—expanded the scope of the conflict beyond a single battlefield. What emerges is not a contained war, but a networked one, stretching across multiple territories and actors.

This form of warfare reflects a broader transformation in how conflicts are conducted in the 21st century. Traditional frontlines are increasingly absent. Instead, violence is distributed—across cities, infrastructure systems, and civilian environments. Strategic targets exist alongside civilian ones, and the distinction between the two becomes increasingly difficult to maintain in practice.

For those within the region, the consequences of this shift are immediate. The outbreak of conflict does not arrive gradually—it interrupts. Normal life gives way to uncertainty within hours. Airspace closures, emergency alerts, and the anticipation of further escalation reshape daily existence.

In the early moments of escalation, what had begun as an ordinary day quickly collapsed into unpredictability. News alerts replaced routine, and within hours, the possibility of returning home became unclear. Airspace closures and missile warnings transformed familiar environments into spaces of risk. Sleep became difficult, time blurred, and attention narrowed to a single question: what happens next?

For civilians, this is the reality of modern war—not strategy, but disruption.

At the same time, the geopolitical implications extend far beyond immediate human impact. The involvement of the United States introduces a global dimension, raising the stakes of escalation. Iran’s regional influence ensures that any confrontation risks spreading across multiple fronts.

Meanwhile, Israel’s security approach—built on rapid and often preemptive action—sustains a pattern in which escalation is not only a reaction, but a calculated choice.

This produces a fragile balance: one where no side intends to trigger full-scale war, yet each decision brings it closer.

Economic consequences further intensify the situation. The Middle East remains central to global energy systems, and instability within the region has immediate effects on international markets.

Yet perhaps the most significant challenge lies in the limitations of international response. Legal frameworks emphasize protection and de-escalation, but enforcement remains inconsistent.

This raises a fundamental question: can modern conflict still be contained?

War, in its modern form, is no longer something that can be observed from afar. It is experienced—suddenly, unpredictably, and often without warning.
